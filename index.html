Learn Swahili for Black Power. In One Year.

Welcome to Kamit Swahili. It's a complete African-Centered Swahili course available online, made by African people for African people. It's designed to help you become proficient in one year.

How? By using an effective combination of practice-driven self-study modules and live weekly Q&A sessions with Swahili teachers from Kenya and Tanzania.

Why Swahili?

With almost 150 million people speaking it as their 1st or 2nd language, Swahili is the most spoken African language in the world. It's an authentic Bantu language that's spoken in the Central African Republic, the Comoros Islands, Malawi, Mozambique, and Northern Zambia.

It's an official language in Tanzania 🇹🇿, Kenya 🇰🇪, Rwanda 🇷🇼, Uganda 🇺🇬 and the Democratic Republic of Congo 🇨🇩.

If we want it, it'll be the common language for the United States of Africa that we're building for all Africans at home and abroad. 💪🏿 💪🏾 💪🏿

How Does The Course Work?

1. Self-study modules

Learn Swahili with a practice-driven approach. Each lesson teaches you a fundamental Swahili grammar concept and then focuses on your listening, speaking, reading, and writing skills.

The content is interactive, fun, and African-Centered, ideal for keeping you motivated. You get brief text explanations, video screencasts, quizzes, and multimedia pronunciation exercises.

2. Weekly Q&A

Every week, we all get on Zoom to interact with our Swahili teachers. It's the perfect opportunity to practice your conversation skills with a native speaker and ask any questions.

We record all the Q&A sessions. When you can't attend, you're still able to watch the recording at your own time.

3. Private Community

Get access to our private online community where you can connect with brothers and sisters who have the same mindset as you.

It's a great way to meet people, ask questions about Swahili or Pan African projects and issues. Our Swahili teachers are also there to help.

What's In The Course?

Three key modules that will take you from beginner to proficient.

Foundations Module 01. Naming Things

In Swahili, there are no masculine or feminine genders. Sounds like good news but there are actually a lot more genders (or classes) than in English, depending on the noun. For example, kiti (= chair) belongs to a different gender or class than mke (= woman).

The noun class system is the foundation of the Swahili language and the most difficult thing for newcomers. In this module of 8 lessons, we'll teach you an intuitive way to grasp how naming works in Swahili. Then we'll help you master the system with engaging interactive exercises.

Foundations Module 02. Describing Things

Once you know how to name things, it'll be time to describe them. In Swahili, the adjectives and different pronouns change depending on the class of the noun they're referring to.

Throughout the 13 lessons of this module, you'll learn to use the different types of adjectives and pronouns, the date and time systems, how to ask questions and compare things. Thanks to the fun interactive exercises, you'll soon be able to describe things confidently.

Foundations Module 03. Making sentences

In this last key module, you'll reach proficiency by learning to produce various types of sentences, using all the tenses and the core grammatical constructs of the Swahili language.

With its 37 lessons, this module is the biggest. In the end, you'll know more than 2000 words. You'll be able to understand the gist of most spoken conversations and written text in Swahili. You'll also be able to express yourself by speaking or writing. You'll be proficient! 🎉

Conversation Modules. Becoming Fluent

After completing the Foundations cycle, you can stay with us to gradually reach the fluency level. The conversation modules are a form of virtual immersion in the Swahili-speaking world. The goal is to broaden your vocabulary, increase your listening, your speaking, your writing, and your reading skills to make you an advanced Swahili speaker.

The conversation modules consist of a weekly personal study of a piece of media or a specific theme (news articles, essays, books, traditional tales, news reports, tv shows, movies/documentaries, speeches, interviews, songs, etc.). The piece is then discussed collectively during a live meeting and Q&A session on Zoom, involving our Swahili teachers and the other members at this level.
